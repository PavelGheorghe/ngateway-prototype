How a domain moves from one registrar to another

This is the usual inter-registrar transfer picture for many gTLDs (for example .com, .net, .org). The registry (runs the TLD) holds the authoritative record; registrars are retail agents. The domain’s DNS can stay on the same nameservers during transfer—only the sponsoring registrar at the registry changes.

Typical steps

  1. At the losing registrar: remove transfer lock, obtain the EPP / auth code, confirm registrant contact email (used for approval notices).
  2. At the gaining registrar: start a transfer, enter the domain and auth code, pay if required.
  3. The registry processes the request; the losing side often emails the registrant (FOA — form of authorization). A pending transfer window applies (often around five days for many gTLDs unless policy or action shortens it).
  4. If not rejected per policy, the registry completes the transfer; the domain is now sponsored by the new registrar (often with a one-year renewal extension on the registration term, per product rules).

ICANN gTLD policy also restricts transfers in some cases—for example within 60 days of initial registration or a prior transfer (with limited exceptions). ccTLDs (country codes) and some TLDs use different rules, interfaces, and timelines.
